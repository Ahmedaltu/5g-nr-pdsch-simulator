FILE: channel/awgn_channel.m
BLOCK: 04 — AWGN Channel Model

WHAT THIS FILE DOES
-------------------
Adds realistic thermal noise to the transmitted signal at a specified SNR.
This is the simplest channel model — the baseline before adding multipath fading.

Position in the simulation pipeline:
  TX chain --> awgn_channel <-- THIS FILE --> RX chain

WHAT A CHANNEL IS
-----------------
In wireless communications, the CHANNEL is everything that happens to
your signal between the transmitter antenna and the receiver antenna.

It includes:
  - Thermal noise (modelled here)
  - Multipath fading (modelled later with TDL-C)
  - Path loss (signal weakens with distance)
  - Doppler shift (signal frequency shifts if transmitter/receiver moving)
  - Interference from other users

AWGN models only the thermal noise component. It is the irreducible
noise floor present in every real receiver.

THE NAME AWGN EXPLAINED
------------------------
ADDITIVE:
  The noise is simply ADDED to the signal: rx = tx + noise
  It is not multiplied or convolved — just added on top.
  This is the simplest possible noise model.

WHITE:
  The noise power is equal at ALL frequencies — flat spectrum.
  This is called "white" by analogy with white light, which
  contains equal amounts of all colours (frequencies).
  In practice, thermal noise is white over the bandwidths used in 5G.

GAUSSIAN:
  The amplitude of the noise follows a NORMAL (Gaussian) distribution.
  At any given moment, the noise is equally likely to be positive or
  negative, and large excursions are exponentially unlikely.
  The central limit theorem ensures that the sum of many random
  processes (which thermal noise is) follows a Gaussian distribution.

WHY AWGN IS OUR BASELINE
--------------------------
1. VALIDATION:
   AWGN has a known theoretical BER (bit error rate) formula:
     BER_QPSK = Q(sqrt(2 * Eb/N0))
   If your simulated BLER curve does not match this theory,
   something is wrong in your TX or RX chain. Fix it before
   adding the complexity of fading channels.

2. SIMPLICITY:
   No channel estimation needed for AWGN — h=1 everywhere.
   You can verify LDPC, modulation, and OFDM work correctly
   before adding the estimation blocks.

3. REFERENCE:
   Published research always shows AWGN curves alongside fading
   curves. The gap between them quantifies how much the channel
   degrades performance and how much your estimator recovers.

THE NOISE VARIANCE FORMULA
----------------------------
For a unit-power signal (mean(|tx|^2) = 1):

  SNR = signal_power / noise_power = 1 / noise_power
  noise_power = 1 / SNR_linear

For COMPLEX noise (which we use because 5G signals are complex):
  The noise has real and imaginary components.
  Each component has variance sigma^2 = noise_power / 2 = 1/(2*SNR_linear)

  So each noise sample = sqrt(sigma^2) * (randn + j*randn)

  The total noise power = sigma^2 (real) + sigma^2 (imag) = 2*sigma^2 = 1/SNR ✓

DECIBELS EXPLAINED
------------------
SNR is expressed in decibels (dB) because wireless signals span many
orders of magnitude and dB keeps the numbers manageable.

  SNR_dB = 10 * log10(SNR_linear)

  Converting back:
  SNR_linear = 10^(SNR_dB / 10)

  Examples:
    0 dB  --> SNR_linear = 1      (signal = noise power)
    3 dB  --> SNR_linear = 2      (signal 2x noise)
   10 dB  --> SNR_linear = 10     (signal 10x noise)
   20 dB  --> SNR_linear = 100    (signal 100x noise)
  -5 dB  --> SNR_linear = 0.316   (noise > signal — very noisy!)

In our BLER sweep we test SNR from -5 dB to +20 dB.
At -5 dB noise dominates — BLER near 1.0 (almost everything fails).
At +20 dB signal dominates — BLER near 0.0 (almost nothing fails).

WHY WE NORMALISE TX POWER FIRST
---------------------------------
The SNR formula assumes signal power = 1.
If we don't normalise, the actual SNR will differ from SNR_dB.

For example: if tx has power 2.5 instead of 1:
  Actual SNR = 2.5 / noise_power = 2.5 * SNR_linear
  This means you're simulating 4 dB more SNR than intended.
  Your BLER curve will be shifted and wrong.

Always normalise before adding noise.

WHAT CHANGES WHEN WE ADD FADING (for NN channel estimator)
-----------------------------------------------------------
AWGN:   rx = tx + noise          (h=1, trivial to equalise)
Fading: rx = h * tx + noise      (h is complex, varies per subcarrier)

The complex channel coefficient h:
  - Different for each subcarrier (frequency selective)
  - Changes over time as the user moves (time varying)
  - Has amplitude fading (|h| < 1 means signal is attenuated)
  - Has phase rotation (angle(h) rotates the constellation)

Estimating h is the job of the LS, MMSE, and NN estimators.
Without estimating h, equalization (dividing by h) is impossible.

MATLAB FUNCTIONS USED
----------------------
  randn(size) -- generates real Gaussian random samples ~ N(0,1)
  abs()       -- complex magnitude
  mean()      -- arithmetic mean

PARAMETERS IN THIS PROJECT
---------------------------
  SNR_range = -5:2:20 dB  (swept in the BLER measurement loop)
  Signal:     unit power after normalisation

THE BLER CURVE CONNECTION
--------------------------
We call awgn_channel once per simulated transport block, with the
same SNR_dB for all blocks at a given SNR point in the sweep.

Low SNR  (-5 dB):  lots of noise, many bit errors, LDPC cannot correct all
                   --> many CRC failures --> high BLER (close to 1.0)

High SNR (+15 dB): little noise, few bit errors, LDPC corrects all
                   --> almost no CRC failures --> low BLER (close to 0.0)

The transition between high and low BLER as SNR increases is the
"waterfall curve" — the main result of the simulator.
